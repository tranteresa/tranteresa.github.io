Trust is an inevitable experience

Have you ever sat down and thought about what Trust is? Or about what it takes to earn your Trust? Consider this one:

What situations do not involve trust?

The answer may not be easily defined or articulated, as it is dynamic and often "just a feeling you get." However, its presence is inevitable. Trust is a conversation that will always be present as long as humans exist to have feelings.

Trust is involved in virtually everything, whether we're conscious of it or not. And design is no exception.

The Challenge

Design a skill-trading community online platform from scratch.

How might we build an environment for others to exchange skills and knowledge online without money? How might we use Time and Skills as a resource?

My role

Full ownership of UX and Product. I was responsible for managing and owning this project solo from scratch all the way to testing the clickable prototype. This included scoping, research, analysis, strategy, requirements, vision, branding, product, wireframing, testing, and more.

Scope

Bartery started off as a product idea. However, Trust became central to its creation after scoping and initial research.

Sifting through uncertainty of viability

Bartery started off as a product idea: An online community for people to exchange skills and knowledge via video chat sessions. Time and skills would be the main currency instead of money.

But before building it out, I had to find out:

  • How useful would this product be?
  • To whom would it be useful?
  • Why would it be useful to them? What purpose does it serve?

To answer these questions, I designed and conducted surveys and interviews with potential users.

The verdict

All users expressed interest in the concept and potential. Some wanted more context (i.e., a tangible application to try out) before forming a concrete opinion about how useful it would be to them. The main purposes it would serve users were for personal growth, lifelong learning, and the potential to save money.

Of course, the concept wasn't without its concerns. A significant one popped up with an interview participant:

What if I feel like I have nothing to offer, but still want to use the service? — Mike, interviewee

Some were more interested in learning, not teaching, which might have something to do with how Mike felt.

The questions piled on:

  • How might we help users feel like they have something to contribute?
  • Will exchanges be a flat (i.e., minute for minute), or will users have the chance to negotiate?
  • What does negotiation involve?
  • How will users select a person to trade with?
  • How might we help users in during that selection process?
  • How might we account for people who only want to learn?

But then, it dawned on me.

The multi-layers of trust were central

Aha! Everything led back to one theme: Trust. People won't feel confident and will likely leave if trust is lacking.

Through my work, I discovered that trust online is a multi-layered interaction. It must be established in three ways:

1. Trust for the product

The product must be consistent and reliable. If anything goes wrong during a trade, user must have confidence that the company will be able to provide fair and sufficient solutions. In other words, user has to feel like itâ€™s got their back.

2. Trust for a potential trader

People will not invest their resources in something or someone they do not trust. So, how do we set both parties up for success?

3. Trust in oneself

If people donâ€™t feel confident they have anything to offer for the trade, they are likely to give up on it. This could mean encouraging users to think outside the box to show them they probably do have something to give!

The Research

To further this exploration and understanding of Trust, I conducted Competitive Analysis (which also included Usability Tests and Card Sorting) and Phone Interviews.

Seeking competitors

In order to gain a better, and quicker, understanding of the problem, I dug around for existing similar products to analyze.

Products were chosen based on the following elements:

  • Have a community where users interacted with one another regularly
  • Involve an element of learning and passing on knowledge.
  • Did not utilize money as a means of transaction between users.
  • Were ideally a trade-based community.

I found various sites like SkillTrade, TradeGig, and Cronnection. I also observed products that were specific to language exchanges, like LRNGO and iTalki. I learned from products that didn't meet all my requirements, such as YouTube.

I used the products to observe and analyze user reviews, user flows, features, branding, trends, and more. It was an opportunity to understand the where, what, why, and how people might be using the product.

However, only one product came close to functionality and similarity to my product idea: TimeRepublik.

(Usability) Testing the competition

Since TimeRepublik was the only fully operating product, I decided to test its usability with potential users. I recruited and met in person with research participants who have never used TimeRepublik before, and had them go through a few tasks:

Usability Test Tasks

  • Task 1: Check out TimeRepublik's landing page.
  • Task 2: Sign Up for an account.*
  • Task 3: Earn your first 5 hours of TimeCoinsâ„¢ — the currency used for trades.

*Users were given dummy email accounts & passwords.

The results

Here is what research participants experienced from the usability test:

  1. Users felt overwhelmed by the amount of features and options available. Learning how to use the product took significantly longer than expected.
  2. The lack of guidance (in addition to the abundance of features) left users frustrated.
  3. Users were required to take many steps that showed no immediate value. What is this? Why is it here? I donâ€™t need this. Itâ€™s in the way.
  4. Difficult learning curve. Users had a difficult time figuring out how to earn the TimeCoinsâ„¢ they were promised with sign-up.
  5. Privacy became a concern once users learned how TimeCoinsâ„¢ were earned, because personal information would have to be given up. Users ended up forfeiting potential earnings for the sake of their privacy. Even if I could keep it private, why would the company need my address and phone number?
  6. There was a lack of feedback confirming completed steps had been registered by site.

If users are already frustrated this early on in their interaction with a product, they are more likely to give up on it. This feedback was incredibly useful in understanding what could affect conversion rates.

Observing how users trust

Cardsorting method

To see how users trust, I looked at how they might form decisions around interacting with other traders. After each usability test, participants were given several Card Sorts that would allow me to observe different points of interactions a user might have with another trader.

Potential points of interaction:

  1. Searching and browsing for a potential trader.
  2. Narrowing down a list of potential traders.
  3. Looking at a potential trader's profile.
  4. Reading reviews about a potential trader.

From this, I created four different card sort categories:

  1. the search pageâ€™s Filter bar â€” to observe what might be initially important to users' when browsing and narrowing.
  2. the search pageâ€™s Sorting options â€” to observe how users might prioritize.
  3. another traderâ€™s Profile Page, and â€” to observe how a users' confidence and trust is built.
  4. types of reviews on another user â€” to observe what further details are important to a user.

Each category above consisted of pre-made cards for participants to prioritize, as well as blank ones to include their own ideas. The categories were presented in the order shown above to mimic a realistic user flow to be used later on.

I found that conducting this after the usability test primed participants to quickly understand the context of the card sort, therefore avoiding unnecessary wasted time, confusion, and fatigue.

Research Conclusion

What affects userâ€™s trust in a product or company?

  • Reliability that the company provide support if anything goes wrong.
  • Giving users relevant help.
  • Consistency in design and in values.
  • Clarity. Material presented must have a clear purpose.
  • Ease of use and presentation.
  • Less mental work. Gives users just the right number of options, instead of saturating it with things they donâ€™t need.

What affects userâ€™s trust in others?

  • The willingness to put in the effort and time to earn trust.
  • Testimonials are powerful. People trust what others have to say about you, not what you have to say about yourself.
  • Proof of existence, such as visuals and level of depth or completeness of profile.
  • A level of transparency. It reads that they have nothing to hide.

What else do we know?

In addition to the research outlined above, I hunted and gathered information on the best design practices. Details on profile searches were more difficult to find. I thought perhaps extracting specific information from e-commerce practices would still be helpful. You can check out the list of notes here. The most important take-aways were:

  • Do the work for the users.
  • Help users get what they need as efficiently as possible.
  • Make suggestions (e.g. Browse similar products).
  • Make sure users always know where they are and what is happening.
  • Inspire return visits.
  • Make it easy and fast.
The Analysis & Strategy

In this phase, Personas were developed based on previous Interviews, with a heavy emphasis on how users trust. The Personas were then used to define and develop User Stories and Flows.

Visualizing trust research

In developing personas, the main goal was to show a snapshot of how trust is lost or earned. I used the previous usability test to showcase the story.

Here was my process.

First, figure out the information architecture of the most pertinent information. Paper and sticky notes were used — color coded, of course.

Second, brainstorm different ways of visualizing trust and user experiences with paper and pencil. Make cause and effects of interactions a priority.

Lastly, visualize trust in personas by displaying a flow on how to gain or lose a typical user's trust.

The Strategy

User Stories & Flows

There are two main user types identified:

  1. The Seeker, someone who is seeking another user to fulfill a need.
  2. The Provider, someone who fulfills a need by providing services

Roles may flip-flop or users may take on both roles simultaneous during a trade, but for the purpose of staying focused on our original user flow, I kept it simple with a couple tweaks.

I focused on the first user type, The Seeker, and their flow of seeking a Provider to meet their needs and completing a Trade Request with a Provider. The Providerâ€™s user flow, stories, and needs were still considered during the process due to the fact that interactions are a two-way street.

Despite the already seemingly simplistic user flow and focus, as I continued, I noticed that things could be simplified even further in a way that would benefit the experience. This realization came when I remembered an important principle from my research:

How might we help users make decisions as easy and quickly as possible?

Themes and requirements

Conducting research and analysis on users brought up various themes that influenced my design decisions, especially when it came to Wireframing and Feature Implementation for Bartery.

The power of testimonials

  • Include a way for people to be ranked and rated in trust
  • Promote helpful reviews
  • Ability to Follow other users

The power of visuals

  • Users must have good quality profile photos
  • Options to upload portfolio or use imagery when listing a service

Other solutions to design problems were also considered:

  • Using only what is needed to avoid overwhelming users
  • An obvious FAQ and help section
  • Guide users to success every step of the way
  • Earn coins in various ways that do not compromise privacy

Implementing features to support trust

Users expressed the importance of showing qualifications and proof of their existence by getting a "window into a person's life."

Useful reviews

Because of it's power and significance in decision-making, reviews and ratings should be designed to be trusted and useful from the get-go.

Set users up for success

As mentioned before, users were concerned that they wouldn't have anything to offer. We can alleviate this concern by the power of suggestions! Suggesting ideas not only increases chances for continued use of the product, it shows what is possible. And when a user's mind is opened up to what's possible, they begin to develop trust in their own capabilities.

To further help the user on their path to success, easy and accessible features were implemented to help them along the way:

Testing & Iterations

To test the progress of my wireframes, I created a clickable prototype on inVision and created a survey on Google Forms that would take participants through the main user flow. Unfortunately, I received very few responses, and my hunch is that the survey and tasks felt too long. Pressed for time, I sought guidance and feedback from my senior mentor and peers.

Initial feedback

  • The flow was quick and easy to understand.
  • Users delighted in unique features of Samaritan and Trust scores.
  • There were two spots that users felt confused about: The booking modules and the search results cards.

Problem spot 1: booking modules

For this particular one, I wanted to see how users would respond if I included minimal explanation of the content present.

At first, it looked like this.

The symbols in the middle were met with confusion and guesses. "Is it online? Does it match my location and availability? Is something on repeat?" Even if users guessed correctly, there is no way for them to confirm it with confidence.

Taking those details into consideration, I explored varying placements and architectures of the symbols, adding additional details to supplement clear understanding.

Afterwards, preference testing was performed and a few more iterations designed, resulting in this improved wireframe.

This new wireframe resulted in reduced cognitive fatigue when scanning content top down instead of zig-zagging side-to-side. Also, space was opened up in the CTAs to avoid crammed information. Bookmarks were moved to a different area.
